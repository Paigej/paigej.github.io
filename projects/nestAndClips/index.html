Nests and Clips


View the STLs on Github

For the nesting objects assignment, I followed Jasper's tutorial on YouTube (thanks Jasper!)


I loved learning about how to manipulate the output of the data and lists in Grasshopper. That was the most useful thing to leverage in the nodegraph to organized the objects together.



In rhino, I adjusted all of the cylinders up so that they could all print within each other (1.5mm of distance apart)



I used support for the space between the cylinders so that I could break them apart.



Here they are disassembled!



For the next part of the project, I used cardboard ~3.91 mm in thickness, although that varied slightly across the board.



I used one curve to create two identical shapes that would act as the two slides of the clip to slide on either side of the cardboard. I used one number slider to place how far I wanted those clips apart (the thickness of the cardboard). That fed both into the distance vector for how far apart the clips were, and also a vector for moving the clips to center around the origin. I started with with the value of the thickness of the cardboard, but after a test print, moved it down to 3.7mm to fit more snug around the board.



I used a polar array with a number slider to adjust how many sides of the clip I wanted. I could create clips from two sides all the way up to 18.



I tried printing all of the clips and once. I learned my lesson about starting a risky print and going to bed. From then on, I added a small brim around the clips and printed them one at a time.



Here is a clip with 8 sides to it.



I used 18 clips to clip these 2.5 inch pieces of cardboard together. I used blocks in Rhino to cut of edges by hand for corner pieces.


The cardboard survived some games of volleyball! I could continue to lower the size of the clip, to make it studier, but it was a trade-off of more stability for crushing the cardboard.
